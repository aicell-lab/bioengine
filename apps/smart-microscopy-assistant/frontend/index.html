Designing a test the model can handle
Works well for
  • Categorical judgements where the difference is visually obvious — presence vs. absence, broad type, gross orientation.
  • Focus and sharpness questions: sharp vs. blurry, in-focus vs. out-of-focus areas.
  • Coarse colour and channel description in fluorescence and brightfield images.
  • Locating clearly distinguishable objects via bounding boxes or points (the model was trained for this and is competitive with specialist detectors on common categories).
  • One yes/no question about a single visually-prominent property.
Struggles with
  • Precise counting. Reliable up to roughly ten distinct objects; large counts drift by 30 %+ in our tests, matching reports in the literature.
  • Reading text. OCR is unreliable on small, low-contrast, rotated, or stylised text — OCRBench v2 places the 3B well behind dedicated OCR models.
  • Fine geometric judgements. Small rotations, angle measurements, sub-millimetre offsets and pixel-level localisation drop below 15 % on segmentation benchmarks.
  • Multi-clause "AND" criteria. When PASS requires several conditions at once the model checks the most prominent one and asserts the rest.
  • 3D spatial relationships. "On top of" / "under" / "behind" are routinely mis-judged, especially when reference images push the wrong way (a well-known anchor-bias failure).
  • Domain-specific patterns it wasn't trained on. Zero-shot detection on novel medical / microscopy categories is below 7 % mAP — references carry most of the signal.
  • Hallucination under ambiguity. When visual evidence is weak the model falls back on language priors and can invent details that aren't there.
Tips for a useful test
  • Keep each test to one axis. Split "focused AND centred" into two separate tests.
  • Phrase criteria as visible properties ("sharp cell outlines", "any saturated pixel"), not abstract quality words ("good image").
  • References do most of the work. Two or three references per side, each covering a distinct failure mode, is the sweet spot. The 5-per-side cap is a ceiling, not a target — five near-duplicates rarely add signal.
  • For domain-specific tasks (e.g. microscopy) the references usually carry more signal than the criterion text — pick them carefully.
  • Always validate on held-out images before relying on a new test.
